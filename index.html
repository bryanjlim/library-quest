Library Quest

Libby lets you borrow ebooks and audiobooks free from your library. Each library system has its own digital collection, so the more eligible cards you collect, the more you can borrow — with shorter waitlists and more ways to read. Library Quest maps every card worth collecting and tracks the ones you've got.

How it works

Find systems you qualify for

California residents can get a card at any public library system in the state — most in person with a CA ID, many online.

Collect the cards

Each card adds that system's collection to Libby. Different systems stock different titles and have separate holds queues.

Borrow more, wait less

More collections means more available copies of the book you want — and a shorter line when there's a waitlist.

States

Maps for states beyond California are coming; meanwhile each card summarizes how library access works there. Statewide access: residents can get a card at nearly any library in the state. Reciprocal: access beyond your home library depends on agreements between systems. Local cards: cards are mostly limited to each library's own service area. Shared catalog: most libraries pool one statewide Libby collection, so extra cards add little.

CA
California
California residents can get library cards at any public library system in the state, unlocking more Libby ebooks and audiobooks.
AL
Alabama
Local cards
Alabama cards are issued by local city and county libraries, many of which pool their ebooks into the shared Camellia Net Libby collection. Large systems like Birmingham-area libraries and regional groups like the Southeast Alabama Digital Library keep separate collections, so a second card only helps if it comes from outside your consortium.
AK
Alaska
Shared catalog
Cards come from your local library (Anchorage, for example, gives free cards only to municipality residents; outsiders pay $50/year), but nearly 60 public, academic, tribal, and school libraries — including Anchorage, Fairbanks, and Mat-Su — share one Alaska Digital Library Libby collection. Since most Alaskans see the same digital catalog, collecting multiple Alaska cards adds little.
AZ
Arizona
Local cards
Arizona cards are county- and city-based: Maricopa County Library District is free for county residents ($50/year for other Arizonans), while Pima County (Tucson) issues free cards to any Arizona resident in person. Eight metro-Phoenix-area systems — including Phoenix, Maricopa County, Mesa, and Scottsdale — share one Greater Phoenix Digital Library collection, so multiple Phoenix-area cards add nothing; a Pima County card is the one that unlocks a genuinely separate Libby catalog.
AR
Arkansas
Local cards
Arkansas cards are issued by county and regional systems to their own residents (Central Arkansas Library System charges outsiders $72 a year). Most public libraries pool their ebooks in the Arkansas Digital Library Consortium's shared Libby collection, so multiple small-library cards add little — the main separate catalog is Central Arkansas Library System's own collection in Little Rock.
CO
Colorado
Statewide access
Colorado's library standards and the Colorado Libraries Collaborate program mean any Colorado resident can get a card at virtually any public library in the state, and most libraries run their own separate Libby collections — so collecting multiple cards really pays off. Note that a few systems add hurdles: since August 2024, Denver Public Library eCard holders living outside Denver lost Libby/OverDrive access and must register in person for a permanent card to use it.
CT
Connecticut
Reciprocal
Through the borrowIT CT program, any Connecticut resident can use their home library card to borrow physical items at 191 public libraries statewide, but that does not extend to ebooks. Libby access stays tied to your home library's consortium (Bibliomation, LION, Library Connection, or an independent collection), so extra Connecticut Libby collections are hard to unlock without qualifying for another system's card.
DE
Delaware
Shared catalog
Delaware's public libraries operate as one system with a single shared catalog, and one library card works everywhere in the state. All libraries share the same statewide Delaware Libraries collection in Libby, so one card is all you need and extra cards add nothing.
FL
Florida
Local cards
Florida cards are issued county by county, and while regional reciprocal agreements (like the Tampa Bay Library Consortium) let you borrow physical books elsewhere, reciprocal cards are excluded from Libby and other digital services. Each county runs its own ebook collection, so extra Florida catalogs usually require paid non-resident cards.
GA
Georgia
Shared catalog
Any Georgia resident can get a free PINES card that works at roughly 300 library branches across 149 counties. For ebooks, most (but not all) PINES systems belong to Georgia Download Destination, a separate Libby consortium of 50+ library systems — your PINES card unlocks it only if your home library is a GADD member. A handful of metro Atlanta systems (Fulton, DeKalb, Cobb, Gwinnett, Forsyth) stay outside PINES and run their own Libby catalogs, so cards there are the main ones worth adding.
HI
Hawaii
Shared catalog
Hawaii runs the only single statewide public library system in the US: one free card for any resident works at all 51 branches on every island, and there's one shared Libby collection behind it. There are no other systems, so there's nothing extra to collect — though non-residents can buy a card ($25 for five years, or $10 for three months) to tap the whole collection.
ID
Idaho
Local cards
Idaho cards are tied to your library district, and out-of-district cards usually cost money (Meridian charges $140/year per household). The state's IDEA program adds a shared supplemental e-book collection, and Libby catalogs are otherwise fragmented across separate consortia (Idaho Digital Consortium, ID8, Valnet, and standalone systems like Boise) — though many of these are OverDrive partner libraries that can borrow from each other's collections, so extra cards help mainly for shorter waits and exclusive titles.
IL
Illinois
Reciprocal
Free cards are limited to residents of each library's taxing district (Illinois residents outside any district must buy a nonresident card), and your home card gives in-person reciprocal borrowing at most Illinois libraries — but that reciprocity doesn't include ebooks. Many libraries pool ebooks in the Digital Library of Illinois, the state's largest OverDrive consortium, alongside other regional pools, while Chicago Public Library runs its own collection.
IN
Indiana
Reciprocal
Cards are limited to your library district, but the statewide PLAC card ($70/year in 2026) lets you borrow in person at any Indiana public library — it excludes ebooks and other digital content, though. Nearly 200 smaller libraries share the Indiana Digital Library on Libby, while large systems like Indianapolis and Allen County keep their own separate collections.
IA
Iowa
Shared catalog
Thanks to the state-funded Open Access program, almost any Iowan can get a card at almost any library in the state — but Open Access cards exclude ebooks and other digital content. It matters little for Libby anyway, since most Iowa libraries share the statewide Bridges collection; only a few metro systems like Des Moines and Cedar Rapids' Metro Library Network run separate collections.
KS
Kansas
Statewide access
Every Kansas resident can get a free State Library of Kansas eCard through any local library, which unlocks statewide digital books (cloudLibrary, Freading, and more — though not Libby). For Libby, about 180 libraries pool one Sunflower eLibrary collection, while larger systems like Johnson County, Wichita, and Topeka run their own — so a Sunflower-member card plus a big-city card genuinely expands your Libby shelf.
KY
Kentucky
Local cards
Kentucky cards are issued by county public libraries to people who live, work, or study locally, and most libraries across the state share one Libby collection called Kentucky Libraries Unbound, run with the state library agency. Because so many libraries pool into that single collection — with Louisville's library as the main separate catalog — collecting multiple Kentucky cards rarely adds new books.
LA
Louisiana
Local cards
Library cards in Louisiana are parish-based, with only patchwork reciprocity — for example, New Orleans issues free cards to Jefferson and St. Bernard parish cardholders, and seven-plus southwest parishes share the Libraries Southwest reciprocal agreement. There's no statewide Libby collection; most parishes buy their own (the Libraries Southwest parishes pool a regional one), so a reciprocal card from a neighboring parish can genuinely add a second Libby catalog.
ME
Maine
Local cards
Maine library cards are issued town by town, with many libraries charging fees for people who live outside their service area. Maine's shared statewide ebook service (the Maine InfoNet Download Library) runs on cloudLibrary rather than Libby, so collecting Maine cards does little for Libby readers.
MD
Maryland
Statewide access
Any Maryland resident can get a free card from any county library system or Baltimore's Enoch Pratt Free Library, no matter where in the state they live. Most systems share the Maryland's Digital Library collection in Libby, but each system also buys its own extra copies and titles, so holding a few cards can still mean shorter waits and some different books.
MA
Massachusetts
Statewide access
Massachusetts residents can register at most public libraries across the state, and anyone who lives, works, or studies in Massachusetts can get a Boston Public Library eCard online with full Libby access. Eight of the state's library networks each run their own Libby collection, but the LEA partnership lets one card borrow from all of them in Libby, so a single card already reaches most of the state's ebooks.
MI
Michigan
Reciprocal
Michigan cards are issued by your home library, and the statewide MILibraryCard program only covers borrowing printed materials in person at participating libraries — not ebooks. There's no single statewide Libby collection, but the regional consortia (Great Lakes Digital Libraries, Up North Digital Collection, Download Destination, and about a dozen others) now interlend through a reciprocal 'partner library' network in Libby, so one Michigan card already reaches many collections.
MN
Minnesota
Reciprocal
Minnesota's Reciprocal Borrowing Compact lets almost anyone with a home library card register and borrow at nearly every other public library in the state — but it covers physical materials only, not ebooks. Libby collections are pooled by region (all Twin Cities metro libraries share MELSA's large collection, other regions have their own), so collecting extra in-state cards rarely unlocks new digital catalogs.
MS
Mississippi
Local cards
Mississippi cards come from regional multi-county library systems, each with its own residency rules, and many of those systems share one Libby collection through the Mississippi eBook Library Partnership. There is no card open to all state residents, so collecting extra Mississippi cards is difficult and often redundant.
MO
Missouri
Reciprocal
Missouri has no statewide card program — access beyond your home library depends on a patchwork of reciprocal agreements, which are generous in the St. Louis and Kansas City metro areas where neighboring systems grant each other's residents full cards. About 120 smaller libraries pool ebooks in Missouri Libraries 2Go, while the big systems (St. Louis County, St. Louis Public, Kansas City, Mid-Continent) each run their own Libby collections, so collecting metro cards can genuinely pay off.
MT
Montana
Shared catalog
You get a card from your local library, but over 100 Montana public, tribal, and academic libraries — including Billings, Missoula, Bozeman, Great Falls, and Helena — all pool one MontanaLibrary2Go Libby collection. The State Library itself notes a resident of tiny Twin Bridges sees the same digital catalog as a Billings resident, so multiple Montana cards add little for Libby.
NE
Nebraska
Reciprocal
Each Nebraska library sets its own card policy, but the NebrasKard program lets borrowers at participating libraries check out from other participating libraries statewide. Nearly 200 libraries share one Nebraska OverDrive Libraries collection in Libby, so extra small-town cards add little — but Omaha and Lincoln run their own separate collections, making those cards the valuable additions.
NV
Nevada
Local cards
Full-access cards are tied to each district — Las Vegas-Clark County's free instant eCard for non-residents notably excludes Libby and hoopla, which require district residency. Any Nevada resident can get a limited Washoe County card that includes online resources, plus a Nevada State Library card with its own small Libby collection, while most rural libraries share the Silver Star eLibrary — so a couple of well-chosen cards can stack separate collections.
NH
New Hampshire
Shared catalog
Cards are issued by each town's library to its own residents, but over 200 of New Hampshire's public libraries pool one shared Libby collection, New Hampshire Downloadable Books, run with the State Library. Since nearly everyone shares the same catalog, collecting multiple New Hampshire cards adds almost nothing.
NJ
New Jersey
Local cards
New Jersey cards are generally limited to people who live, work, or study in a library's own town or county, with annual fees for outsiders and only scattered county-to-county reciprocal agreements. Dozens of libraries share the eLibraryNJ Libby collection, but big consortia like BCCLS run separate collections (eBCCLS) and typically block digital access for non-resident cardholders.
NM
New Mexico
Local cards
Cards are generally issued by each city or county library, though Albuquerque/Bernalillo County — the state's largest system — will issue a card to any New Mexico resident who applies in person. Dozens of smaller libraries share the New Mexico Library To Go collection in Libby, while Albuquerque runs its own separate collection, so pairing a local card with an Albuquerque card pays off.

NC
North Carolina
Reciprocal
Much of North Carolina belongs to NC Cardinal, a consortium where one card works at any member library, and many systems issue free cards to any state resident. Ebooks are split among the NC Digital Library consortium, the e-iNC consortium, and independent big-city systems like Wake and Charlotte, so collecting cards from systems in different consortia unlocks different Libby catalogs.
ND
North Dakota
Shared catalog
The North Dakota State Library and the majority of the state's public libraries share one Libby collection, and any North Dakota resident can get a free State Library card (apply online) that taps that same shared pool. Fargo runs its own separate Libby collection, so a Fargo card is the main extra unlock for Fargo residents.
OH
Ohio
Statewide access
Any Ohio resident can get a free card at essentially any public library in the state. About 175 libraries share The Ohio Digital Library on Libby, but big metro systems like Cleveland (CLEVNET), Cuyahoga County, and Columbus run their own separate collections — so collecting a few metro cards genuinely unlocks extra Libby catalogs.
OK
Oklahoma
Local cards
Free cards are tied to where you live, work, or go to school — Metropolitan Library (Oklahoma City area) and Tulsa City-County sell cards to other Oklahomans for about $65–70 a year. Many smaller libraries share the OK Virtual Library collection in Libby, but Metro, Tulsa, and Pioneer each run separate collections, so extra cards do unlock new books if you can qualify.
OR
Oregon
Reciprocal
Cards are local, but the Oregon Library Passport Program lets cardholders of 100+ participating libraries get free cards at each other's systems, and Portland-metro residents have their own reciprocal agreements. Many libraries share the Library2Go Libby collection, but big systems like Multnomah County, Washington County (WCCLS), and Deschutes run separate collections — so collecting cards can genuinely expand your Libby shelf.
PA
Pennsylvania
Reciprocal
With the Access Pennsylvania program, a home library card lets you sign up for cards at more than 2,000 participating libraries statewide, though each library decides whether those cards include ebook access. In late 2025 the state's POWER Library launched a pilot Libby collection open to all Pennsylvanians, and the Free Library of Philadelphia issues free cards, with a large Libby collection, to any state resident, so collecting Pennsylvania cards can genuinely pay off.
RI
Rhode Island
Shared catalog
One card from any Rhode Island public library works at all of the state's roughly 50 Ocean State Libraries members, and they all share a single Libby collection called the eZone. One Rhode Island card gets you everything, so there is nothing extra to collect.
SC
South Carolina
Local cards
South Carolina cards are generally limited to residents of each county system, though the SCLENDS consortium lets 22 mostly smaller systems (plus the State Library) share one catalog for physical books. Libby collections are run separately by each county system (Richland, Charleston, Greenville, etc.), so extra cards help — if you can qualify for them.
SD
South Dakota
Shared catalog
Eighty libraries statewide share the South Dakota Titles To Go collection on Libby, and as of February 2026 a reciprocal lending agreement links it with the Black Hills Digital Library Consortium and Siouxland Libraries (Sioux Falls). That means nearly any South Dakota card can already reach all three digital pools through Libby's partner-library feature, so collecting extra cards adds little.
TN
Tennessee
Shared catalog
Most Tennessee public libraries share one statewide Libby collection, Tennessee R.E.A.D.S., run by the State Library and Archives — so one card from any participating library covers most of the state's ebooks. The big metro systems (Nashville, Memphis, Knoxville, Chattanooga) are by law not eligible for R.E.A.D.S. and run their own collections, but their cards' digital access is generally limited to local residents.
TX
Texas
Local cards
Most Texas libraries only issue free cards to their own service area, but there are big exceptions: any Texas resident can get a free Houston Public Library MY Link card (online or in person) and a free Harris County Public Library card in person. There's no statewide Libby collection — each major system buys its own — so Texans who collect cards from open systems like Houston and Harris County get real extra Libby access.
UT
Utah
Local cards
Utah cards are issued by your local city or county library, with paid non-resident cards elsewhere (e.g., Salt Lake County charges $15/month up to $180/year for outsiders). Dozens of libraries share one Beehive Library Consortium Libby collection, but the biggest systems — Salt Lake County, Salt Lake City, Davis, Cache, and Murray — run their own separate collections, so extra cards from those systems do unlock new books.
VT
Vermont
Shared catalog
Vermont cards come from your town library, but most libraries statewide share a single Libby catalog through the Green Mountain Library Consortium, and the state also offers ebooks to everyone via the Palace Project app. One Vermont card gets you the shared collection, so extra cards add little.
VA
Virginia
Reciprocal
Virginia library cards are issued system by system, but access varies widely: Northern Virginia systems honor DC-metro reciprocal agreements, and some systems (like Virginia Beach) offer free cards to any Virginia resident, while others charge non-resident fees. There is no statewide Libby collection, so each new card you can get unlocks a separate ebook catalog.

WV
West Virginia
Shared catalog
Any West Virginia resident can get a free statewide card from the West Virginia Library Commission, and most public libraries pool their ebooks into two shared Libby collections: WV Reads and WVDELI (the West Virginia Downloadable Entertainment Library Initiative). Beyond having a card for each of those two collections, gathering more West Virginia cards adds little.
WI
Wisconsin
Shared catalog
Every public library system in Wisconsin shares one statewide Libby collection, Wisconsin's Digital Library, available to all valid Wisconsin cardholders. Any single Wisconsin library card unlocks the whole thing, so collecting extra in-state cards adds nothing for Libby.
WY
Wyoming
Shared catalog
Wyoming's WYLD network links every public library in the state, and they all share one statewide Libby collection (the Wyoming Virtual Library, spanning all 23 county libraries plus the State Library, University of Wyoming, and community colleges) that any Wyoming library cardholder can use. One Wyoming card gets you everything — collecting additional Wyoming cards won't unlock more books.
